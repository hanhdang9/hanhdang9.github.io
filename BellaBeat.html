BellaBeat: How Can a Wellness Technology Company Play It Smart?

I. Case study description:

Bellabeat is a high-tech company that manufactures health-focused smart products empowering women with knowledge about their own health and habits by collecting data on activity, sleep, stress, and reproductive health.

The Bellabeat’s founder knows that an analysis of Bellabeat’s available consumer data would reveal opportunities for growth. She has asked the marketing analytics team to focus on a Bellabeat product and analyze smart device usage data in order to gain insight into how people are already using their smart devices. Then, using this information, she would like high-level recommendations for how these trends can inform Bellabeat marketing strategy.

II. Ask

  1. Identify the business task: analyze smart device usage data in order to gain insight into how consumers use non-Bellabeat smart devices, then apply those insights to Bellabeat’s product.
  2. To do the task, the analysis needs to answer these questions:
  1. What are some trends in smart device usage?
  2. How could these trends apply to Bellabeat customers?
  3. How could these trends help influence Bellabeat marketing strategy?

○ Urška Sršen: Bellabeat’s cofounder and Chief Creative Officer.

III. Prepare

The data to be used in this case study is FitBit Fitness Tracker Data (CC0: Public Domain, dataset made available through Mobius): This Kaggle data set contains personal fitness tracker from thirty fitbit users. Thirty eligible Fitbit users consented to the submission of personal tracker data, including minute-level output for physical activity, heart rate, and sleep monitoring. It includes information about daily activity, steps, and heart rate that can be used to explore users’ habits.

This dataset has 18 files organized in long format.

Check the dataset’s credibility:

  • Reliable: LOW. The dataset contains information from 30 users, with this small sample, the data could have sampling bias since doesn’t represent the population as a whole. Beside, the dataset doesn’t include info about gender of those users, using this to have insight with Bellabeat’s clients – only female - could misleading then.
  • Original: LOW. The “Provenance” session links to a website with this dataset and said: “These datasets were generated by respondents to a distributed survey via Amazon Mechanical Turk” but actually we can’t locate the original data source.
  • Comprehensive: LOW. The dataset is missing some important information needed, for example the height, reproductive health, diet and nutrition, hydration,…
  • Current: LOW. The dataset was generated between 03.12.2016-05.12.2016, more than 7 years ago. This is a long period of time which people might change their diet, routine, health and update with many more knowlegde about daily activities, as well as the significant variation of technology and it’s application to those activities. This could make the insight from this analysis is out of date.
  • Cited: LOW: we couldn’t say exactly the original data source and it’s been a long time since the data was last refreshed.

Licensing, privacy, security, and accessibility: this dataset is under CC0: Public Domain License, means we can copy, modify, distribute and perform the work with this dataset, all without asking permission. For data privacy, this notebook applied Apache License, so I would be protected with my private data over this work.

Let’s explore if the dataset is integrity or not!

  • Since exploring the dataset, I found that the dailyActivity_merged includes dailyCalories_merged; dailyIntensities_merged; dailySteps_merged. I’ve loaded those files into Excel and used Conditional Formating function to define the those files of data are consistent.

  • Other problem of this dataset is that there is no meta data. Actually we need detail descriptions about every column in every file of data to understand clearly about what is the data about. Lacking of that information, there are some files that we couldn’t use since not sure about the meaning of data (minuteSleep_merged, hourlyIntensities_merged, minuteIntensitiesNarrow_merged, minuteIntensitiesWide_merged)

  • Base on what we found, we note that we might have sampling bias problem and we will have a general operational approach on this case study. I pretend to analyse users’ activities till daily, so I will choose below dataset for the analysis: dailyActivity_merged: contains daily totals for steps, intensity, distance, and calories; heartrate_seconds_merged.csv; sleepDay_merged; weightLogInfo_merged.   1. How active are the users?

I would categorize users by their active level (considering to their mean calories burned per day) to see the trend of users’ activity: • Sedentary: less than 1800 calories per day • Moderately active: ranging from 1,800 to 2,400 calories per day. • Active: more than 2400 calories per day.

user_type <- daily_activity_merge %>%
  group_by(id) %>%
  summarise(mean_calories = mean(calories)) %>%
  mutate(user_type = case_when(

    mean_calories >= 1800 & mean_calories <= 2400 ~ "moderately active", 
    mean_calories > 2400 ~ "active")) %>%
  group_by(user_type) %>%
  summarise(percentage = n()/nrow(.)) %>%

VI. Recommendation

  1. As we see within the small and poor dataset, this shows clearly that users are not willing to share the whole or a part of their health/daily activity to others. This seems obvious but we could emphasize our policy of keeping customers’ data private and tell about our feature of deactive the app (as well as delete the data) on whatever devices the customers want to show how we care about customers’ data privacy.

  2. As almost users didn’t log their activities regularly, the marketing team has the opportunity to underscore the device’s attributes of being lightweight, wearable, and self-operating. This way, users can comfortably wear the device throughout the day, enabling continuous 24/7 tracking without requiring any additional interventions.

  3. Given that a substantial portion of users acknowledges the significance of exercise, yet their engagement appears to lack consistency, an effective strategy to enhance their motivation and commitment to workouts could involve establishing individualized goals, implementing incentive-based rewards, and fostering a sense of community within the exercise realm.

  4. Given the suboptimal quality of users’ sleep, the marketing team has the potential to accentuate the profound impact of restful sleep on overall well-being. This approach could highlight how a tracker can assist in monitoring both sleep patterns and overall health, thereby underscoring the device’s value.

  5. With health organizations and experts underscoring the significance of diminishing sedentary habits and integrating consistent physical activity into daily routines to enhance overall well-being, the marketing team possesses an opportunity to highlight the health hazards associated with a sedentary lifestyle. By presenting viable solutions, they can illustrate how our tracker aids in raising awareness about these risks, enabling users to manage and enhance their health by curtailing sedentary behaviors.
